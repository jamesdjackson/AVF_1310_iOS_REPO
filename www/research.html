2. Visit Programmableweb.com and search for JSON-P data sources. Why would we need JSON-P, specifically?

JSON-P is essentially a work around to allow an application to retrieve data form an external domain. This would ordinarily be treated as a cross domain violation and not be allowed. However, the request is sent as JavaScript and handled in such a way that the data is allowed to be retrieved or interpreted as script, rather than parsed as a JSON data set.

Accessibility

1. What are some of the ways that you could change your HTML on your Demo app to be more accessible? (Remember, you can use the Section tag with jQuery, for instance)

A search on Google returned an interesting presentation from Stanford discussing accessibility. They identified four areas of concern, or in their words; “impairments that can negatively impact users on the web.”

They identified the most prevalent Assistive APIs (AAPIs) such as:

• Microsoft Active Accessibility (MSAA) (Microsoft)

• IAccessible2 (Linux Foundation)

• User Interface Automation (UIA) (Microsoft)

• Linux Accessibility Toolkit (ATK) and Assistive Technology - Service Provider Interface (AT-SPI) (gnome.org)

• Mac OS X Accessibility Protocol (Apple)

They went on to discuss common markup syntax that can be employed by these APIs such as specific assistive roles in the markup which can be embellished to provide more context and utility for impaired users. The presentation also stressed semantic the use of semantic structure, again to better describe the object and provide context to the accessibility tools and ultimately the user.

I cite all of these things, because they are all things I believe I can improve in my own overall practice, not just this project. I think beyond just accessibility, they are good practices to keep.

2. Visit a video service on the web (like YouTube) and search for "Screen Reader Demo" and "Mobile Screen Reader" - what are some of the common frustrations (or common support mechanisms) that you see among the examples that are given - how could you fix them (or use them)?

I do not believe it is a matter of “fixing” the apps for assistive technology as much as it is developing discreet platforms for impaired users. I believe we are trying to make the best truck we can out of a car, rather than just starting off with a truck.

We have evolved the usage, ergonomics, and usability over many years for mainstream, non-impaired users. Why should there even be a gui for blind users? Why not an interface based upon sound or tactile feedback purely?

SO, I guess my answer to improving these solutions or modifications to existing systems would be to forgo them. Stsart from the ground up, and develop based upon the sole needs of the userbase.

3. Devices: Please list two devices (and some of their features) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

I work in the government and we pay close attention to accommodation. I have seen many devices used in the workplace. Common examples are adapted keyboards; which may have larder or more accessible keys or alternate input devices. On-screen keyboards, ubiquitous in mobile computing these days, started off as an accessibility tool back in the day. In fact you can pretty much conjure an on-screen keyboard through the accessibility panel of any version of MS Windows or Mac OS. OCR(Optical character Recognition) Voice Recognition, are all considered assistive technology as well.
